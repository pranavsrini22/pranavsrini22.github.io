NSF Research Internship

As an NSF Research Intern over the summer of 2023, I conducted FEA analysis to simulate impact loads that various components experienced as the arctic buoy that was being designed fell from a flight and impacted the ice. The video shown above depicts an impact simulation on a bottom cap that I designed and refined as it experiences the shock of impacting the Arctic ice.

The simulation shown above was useful in understanding how the component behaves during impact and which features of the component are likely to fail under load. The hole in the middle and the temperature probe slot experienced the largest stresses, and the design of the part had to be modified with optimized fillets and extra supports to ensure the bottom cap would survive the impact while maintaining a watertight seal.

In addition to running impact simulations, I also designed a flexible hull prototype as a solution to reduce impact forces using TPU and conducted some preliminary testing with an accelerometer test kit that I put together. This test kit is also shown above. While impact g-forces were reduced due to the flexible hull, demonstrating that the design is viable, further testing and development is needed to develop a scaled up solution for a larger payload with instrumentation to survive the impact.

Skills: SolidWorks Simulation, Autodesk Fusion 360, 3D Printing

BAC Engineering Systems Internship

Above is a presentation poster that I designed to assist in giving a talk at the College Park Scholars Program's academic showcase. The presentation was an explanation of the work I completed as an intern at the Baltimore Aircoil Company, which the poster goes into detail explaining. The second image shown above is a picture of the cooling tower model, the FXV, whose side and bottom panels I worked on.

As an Engineering Systems Intern, I was tasked with redesigning the Pre-Punch program, a system used to generate hole patterns on the side and bottom panels of BAC's cooling towers for mounting/connecting various pipes and other components. The existing system was a text editor that generated hole patterns by editing the DXF manufacturing files of the panels to include new hole patterns. The new system that I designed, which was to be integrated into a larger design automation program called Genie, aimed to shift the process to Autodesk Inventor using the Inventor API to integrate Pre-Punch into Genie and update the process using newer methods.

My redesigned Pre-Punch program identifies what hole patterns need to be created and where they need to be placed by parsing through configuration files generated by the cooling tower configurator that customers can use to customize their cooling tower order. After properly identifying the hole patterns, the correct hole pattern iFeature is selected from a feature library that I created on Autodesk Inventor, and places the hole pattern on the correct panel in the correct position. These panels can be found in the FXV unit shown above at the bottom of the tower. After the necessary edits, the system exports a dimensioned drawing, the customized part file for the edited side panels, and the DXF manufacturing files used to manufacture the final panels.

Skills: Autodesk Inventor Sheet Metal, Autodesk Inventor API, VB.NET

JHUAPL ASPIRE Internship - Networking Over the Cloud

The image shown above is the CORE (Common Open Research Emulator) GUI (Graphical User Interface) with a network emulation. My project as an ASPIRE Intern at APL was to test the limits of the CORE emulation software given certain specification constraints.

In this project, I developed an emulation of a military brigade communicating in a battlefield with thousands of devices communicating with each other wirelessly. The brigade emulation initially consisted of eight battalions, each with hundreds of devices organized into platoons and companies. Each battalion was assigned a separate VM (Virtual Machine) and the battalions communicated with each other over the cloud.

The purpose of this project was to test the limits of CORE's emulation capabilities (if there were any) as well as to understand the limitations that the specifications of our VMs created on our emulation capabilities. My testing (by running traffic through the emulated network) concluded that only three battalions, consisting of approximately 1800 "soldiers" or devices, could function given the specification constraints of our VMs, and larger emulations would require Vms with much larger memories and greater computing power.

Skills: Python, Ubuntu, CORE
